One and a Part

  1. Home
  2. One and a Part

She may be dead and buried in the ground. She may be bathing and sleeping no more. But she lived a complicated life. And this is her story.

Once, she was an engineer, selling her mind for everything and earning nothing; now, she sells her body, working on her own terms. Once, she had a family, with children and grandchildren; now, they’ve all fallen apart. Once, she had a caring friend; now, even she’s abandoned her. Someday, they will forgive each other and remember her story, but only once that story has been told - when she’s dead and buried beneath her willow tree.

Through its poetry, Collins’ play challenges its audience to think about the complexities of tragedy within family life and womanhood more broadly; raising numerous questions about the value of beauty, trauma, and forgiveness.

Varsity


With its slow-reveal style, this piece has the audience unsure of what is about to happen next. Each answer leaves more questions, which are only finally resolved at the play’s climax. The audience is asked to understand and respect the mother’s decisions, but also to relate with the daughter, and to sympathise with the narrator caught in the middle. In this play, none of the central characters in the family are right or wrong, leaving the audience to decide the truth.

This piece tackles difficult, and sometimes overlooked, topics and explores differing views on sex, gender, friendship, and family relationships. Many moments of the play are delivered through the narrator; all of the information he relates to the audience is filtered by his interpretation and ignorance, letting the audience sort fact from exaggeration.
